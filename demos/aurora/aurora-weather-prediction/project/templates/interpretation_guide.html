How to Interpret Aurora Weather Visualizations

This guide helps you understand the weather visualizations created by the Aurora AI Foundation Model. Each variable uses a specific color scheme to represent different weather conditions.

Note: Aurora is an AI-based weather forecasting model that predicts meteorological variables like temperature, wind, and humidity across a global grid. The color maps represent the intensity and direction of these variables.

2m Temperature (K)

This variable shows the air temperature at 2 meters above the surface in Kelvin. The temperature scale uses a traditional "cold-to-hot" color scheme.

Converting Kelvin to Celsius: To convert from Kelvin (K) to Celsius (°C), subtract 273.15 from the Kelvin value.
Example: 293K = 293 - 273.15 = 19.85°C

This variable represents the mass of water vapor per mass of air. It is a ratio with no units, though often expressed as kg/kg. Higher values indicate more moisture in the air.

Humidity Context: Specific humidity varies with temperature and pressure. The same specific humidity will feel much more humid in cool conditions than in hot conditions. Tropical regions typically have values around 0.015-0.020 kg/kg, while desert regions may have values below 0.005 kg/kg.

Reading the Weather Maps

When interpreting Aurora weather prediction maps, consider these tips:

  • Look for patterns and gradients - Sharp color changes often indicate weather fronts or boundaries between air masses.
  • Compare sequential time steps - The movement of patterns across time steps shows how weather systems are predicted to evolve.
  • Consider all variables together - Temperature, wind, and humidity patterns often relate to each other.
  • Check the color scale values - The specific numerical range for each visualization may vary depending on the global conditions.
Scientific Units: Aurora uses standard meteorological units: Kelvin (K) for temperature, meters per second (m/s) for wind components, and kilograms per kilogram (kg/kg) for specific humidity.
